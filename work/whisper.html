Whisper BI is a conversational Business Intelligence platform that lets SMEs and enterprise teams query their own data in plain language - no SQL, no analyst dependency, no 48-hour wait for a report. 01 · The problem

Business Intelligence is powerful. It's also completely inaccessible to the people who need it most.

The BI market is dominated by platforms built for data teams - Tableau, Power BI, Looker. They require technical configuration, dedicated analysts, and weeks of setup before a business owner sees their first insight. For the SME decision-maker who needs to know why last quarter's margin dropped, the tools available are either too complex to use independently or too shallow to trust.

Whisper BI was built on a different premise: what if the interface itself was the analyst? Instead of drag-and-drop dashboards, a conversational AI that interprets natural-language questions about connected data sources - ERP, CRM, spreadsheets - and responds with structured analysis, charts, and proactive alerts. Not a question-and-answer chatbot. A system that notices things before you ask.

The competitive gap we were exploiting

Enterprise BI tools optimize for data depth and configurability - they assume a technical user. Consumer AI tools optimize for general knowledge - they assume an anonymous user. The gap between them is the business analyst persona: someone with domain expertise, real data, and no time to write SQL. That was our target user, and no one was building for them at SME price points.

02 · Research

Two users, one interface - and they want opposite things.

[My call] Before writing a single spec, I ran a JTBD-framed discovery with two distinct user types we'd identified through early sales conversations: Data Analysts and Business Executives. The hypothesis was that they'd want the same product. The research showed they wanted fundamentally different things from it.

JTBD · Profile A

The Data Analyst

Job-to-be-done: "Produce the report my manager asked for without spending two days cleaning data first." Pain: context-switching between tools, re-doing the same calculations. They wanted depth, control, and export. They could tolerate complexity if it saved them time downstream.

JTBD · Profile B

The Business Executive

Job-to-be-done: "Tell me if something is wrong before I find out in a meeting." Pain: dependency on the analyst, waiting for reports, not knowing what questions to ask. They wanted proactivity, plain language, and decisions - not data. They had zero tolerance for complexity.

Architecture decision

One product, two modes

[My call] The insight that shaped the product: both users could share the same conversational interface if the response type was configurable. "Análisis rápido" for the executive who needs a sentence. "Análisis detallado" for the analyst who needs the breakdown. "Tablero" for the manager who wants to pin it to a dashboard. Same question, three output formats.

Why conversational and not dashboards

The temptation in BI is to build dashboards - they look impressive in demos. But dashboards answer the questions you thought to ask last month. The research showed both user types were routinely surprised by their own data: a margin they thought was stable wasn't, a region they'd ignored was outperforming. The most valuable insight is the one you didn't know to request. That's why we built a proactive alert system on top of the chat interface - the system surfaces anomalies even when the user isn't actively querying.

03 · Product design

The dashboard is a response, not a starting point.

Traditional BI puts the dashboard first. You configure charts, select date ranges, pick metrics. Then you try to read meaning into what you see. Whisper BI inverts this: you ask the question first, and the system decides the best format to answer it. When the answer warrants a chart, it generates one. When it warrants a table, it builds one. When it warrants a number and an explanation, it gives you that.

[My call] The dashboard view is a child of the conversation - generated on demand, accessible via "Volver al chat," and optionally pinned. This kept the mental model clean: the chat is where insight lives, the dashboard is where it gets promoted to permanent reference.

AI-assisted design workflow

[My call] Beyond the product itself, I used AI-assisted design (Claude + Figma integration) to accelerate the creation of UI variants, report templates, and interface personalization assets. This reduced content production time by 60% - which on a bootstrapped timeline translates directly to more research cycles and faster iteration.

04 · Onboarding design

The hardest question in conversational BI: what do I ask first?

The first-use problem in conversational interfaces is severe: users who don't know what to ask drop off immediately. They open the chat, see a blank input, and close the tab. The research showed that the anxiety isn't about the tool - it's about the data. Users worried their question was "wrong," that it wouldn't connect to their actual files, or that they'd waste time configuring something they didn't understand.

[My call] The solution was a 7-step progressive onboarding that converted the blank-screen problem into a guided tour - not a product demo, but a series of micro-tasks that produced real output. By step 3, the user had already received an AI-generated analysis of their own data. By step 5, they'd seen a chart. By step 7, they'd set up their first alert. The onboarding was the first successful use of the product.

How the 40% lift was measured

We defined "onboarding conversion" as reaching step 7 and then initiating a first unprompted query within the same session. The baseline (pre-redesign flow) was a single welcome modal and then direct exposure to the chat interface. Conversion: 28%. After the 7-step progressive onboarding: 40%. The measurement was done via session event tracking - no artificial completion incentive, no prompted query, no pre-filled input. Users who reached step 7 and then queried their own data spontaneously.

05 · Go-to-market design

The landing page is a research artifact.

On a bootstrapped timeline, the marketing site isn't just a sales tool - it's the fastest way to test whether your value proposition resonates before you've built the full product. [My call] I designed the landing page as a research instrument: every section was a hypothesis. "Optimizar Costos / Aumentar Ventas / Reducir Riesgos" tested whether the business impact framing worked better than feature-first messaging. The cases of use ("Casos de Éxito") section tested whether social proof with specific metrics converted better than generic testimonials.

Pricing as a product decision

[My call] I defined the pricing model and feature packaging (unit economics: LTV/CAC) to maximize runway in a bootstrapped environment. The key tension: pricing low enough to attract SME trial, high enough to signal enterprise credibility. The landing page "Inteligencia Proactiva a un Precio Accesible" headline was the output of that tension - it names both the aspiration (proactive intelligence) and the qualifier (accessible price) in a single phrase, positioning against enterprise BI without attacking it directly.
