Case 07 / 07 · 2006 - 2013 · Government · Public Finance · Citizen Experience

Designing for citizens before "UX" was a job title in Mexico.

FOVISSSTE is Mexico's federal housing credit fund for government employees - one of the largest mortgage institutions in the country, operating under SHCP oversight and federal transparency mandates. For seven years, from 2006 to 2013, I led the digital experience of an institution that served millions of citizens who had no alternative: if FOVISSSTE's portal failed them, there was no competitor to switch to.

01 · Context

Designing for a captive audience is the hardest brief there is.

In commercial product design, a poor user experience loses you a customer. In government financial services, a poor user experience loses a citizen access to housing credit they may have been saving toward for years. The stakes of getting it wrong were not measured in churn rates - they were measured in people unable to navigate a mortgage application, losing priority in a queue, missing a documentation deadline.

This is what I mean when I say regulated environments shaped how I think. Not just compliance checkboxes - the actual moral weight of designing for people who can't go elsewhere. FOVISSSTE was where I learned that accessibility isn't a feature. In a federal institution, it was a legal obligation. WCAG 2.0 compliance was mandated by federal digital standards. Every citizen-facing touchpoint had to meet it. There was no design decision that could override that constraint.

The regulatory stack

FOVISSSTE operated under SHCP (Secretaría de Hacienda y Crédito Público) financial oversight, the Ley Federal de Transparencia y Acceso a la Información (LFTAIPG), federal government digital standards, and mandatory WCAG 2.0 web accessibility compliance. Every content decision, every information architecture choice, every form design had a legal reference point. Learning to navigate that stack early - understanding which constraints were hard limits and which were implementation choices - is directly traceable in how I approach compliance review today.

02 · The work

Seven years. Three products. One principle.

The principle was simple to state and hard to execute: every citizen who needed information about their housing credit should be able to find it, understand it, and act on it - regardless of their device, their connection speed, or their disability status. In 2006, that was not the starting condition.

Institutional portal

The face of the institution for millions

Led the redesign of the main institutional portal - the primary digital touchpoint for government employees checking their housing credit eligibility, mortgage status, and account information. The redesign addressed navigation failures that were generating significant call center load: citizens couldn't find their credit status without calling. Post-redesign, the most-searched information was accessible in two clicks from the homepage.

Mobile application

First in the organization's history

Designed and launched FOVISSSTE's first mobile application - at a time when government institutions in Mexico had no mobile presence. The scope was deliberately narrow: credit status lookup, payment schedule, and document checklist. Not because we couldn't imagine more, but because a narrow scope done reliably was more valuable to citizens than a broad scope done inconsistently.

WCAG 2.0 compliance

Legal mandate, not optional feature

Implemented WCAG 2.0 standards across all citizen-facing digital touchpoints. This meant: proper heading hierarchy, sufficient color contrast, keyboard navigability, screen reader compatibility, and alt text for every non-decorative image. The compliance audit was formal, documented, and reviewed by federal accessibility standards bodies. The 2011–2012 recognition from the Presidency cited specifically the improvement in citizen information accessibility.

What research looked like in 2006

There were no Maze accounts. No Hotjar. No Useberry. User research at FOVISSSTE meant: call center log analysis to understand what citizens couldn't find online, manual session observations with government employees using the portal, and iterative design reviews with legal and communications teams who served as proxies for the regulatory constraints. The methods were primitive by today's standards. The discipline of asking "does this actually work for the person who needs it" was not.

03 · Why this matters now

The foundation everything else is built on.

Every senior person who has spent their career in fintech or banking has a story about where they learned that compliance and usability aren't enemies - that regulatory constraints, properly understood, are design constraints like any other. FOVISSSTE is mine.

Designing for citizens who couldn't opt out taught me something that proved true in every subsequent engagement: when you remove the market exit - when the user truly has no alternative - you remove the safety net that normally catches bad design before it causes harm. That sharpens your attention in a specific way. The habit of asking "what happens to the person who can't use this?" isn't something I adopted at Compartamos or Santander. It came from here.

The WCAG work was my first sustained engagement with accessibility as a system - not a checklist, but a set of principles for how information hierarchy, semantic structure, and visual design interact with the full range of human capability. That engagement shaped how I think about design systems, information architecture, and the relationship between visual design and underlying structure ever since.

On the recognition

The formal recognition from the Presidency of the Republic in 2011–2012 was for improved citizen information access - not for visual design. The government evaluated it on functional terms: could citizens find what they needed? The answer, evidently, improved enough to be worth noting. I include it not as a credential but as a calibration: in government digital services, success looks different from commercial success, and it should.
