Case 04 / 06 · 2014 — 2015 · Banking · Internal CRM · Enterprise UX

Five legacy tools. One CRM. Nobody trained for it.

Santander Mexico's credit executives were switching between 5+ command-based systems to handle a single client request — credit simulators, transaction histories, portfolio trackers, training modules, product catalogs. All separate. All inconsistent. The bank had tried to patch this for years. Neo Jupiter was the attempt to fix it properly: one unified CRM, redesigned from research up, adopted nationwide.

Role UX Architect & UI Department Manager
Client / Context Santander Mexico · via Leo Burnett · CNBV-regulated banking environment
Team Sole UX Researcher · 3 UI Designers · 2 Tech Leads · Stakeholders from Branch, Regional, and Executive levels
Headline impact +8% cross-selling · -30% training time · Nationwide adoption replacing legacy Jupiter system

01 · Context

The agency knew advertising. The bank knew regulation. Neither knew the users.

Leo Burnett is one of the most recognizable advertising agencies in the world — responsible for cultural icons from Kellogg's cereal mascots to the Marlboro Man. Their production quality and brand discipline were exceptional. But when the engagement shifted from marketing to designing a banking CRM, the gap showed quickly: deep expertise in brand expression doesn't automatically translate to enterprise UX for regulated financial tools.

[My role] I was brought in to bridge that gap. The project needed someone who could speak both languages — technical enough to understand the capabilities and constraints of the legacy systems being replaced, and UX-fluent enough to translate those constraints into a product that real bank employees would actually use without a manual.

The regulatory layer

This wasn't a product you could ship and iterate on freely. Every data display decision, every access control, every client history view had to pass through Internal Audit and comply with CNBV's customer data handling requirements and Santander's global IT security policies. The design wasn't just a UX problem — it was a compliance problem that also had to be a good UX problem.

02 · Research

The real problem wasn't the systems. It was the mental model.

Before any design work, I ran a multi-method research phase with three types of participants: frontline credit executives who used the tools daily, branch and regional directors who supervised and needed visibility without operational detail, and bank executives at the strategic level who had the authority to approve — and whose buy-in was necessary for nationwide adoption.

Stakeholder workshops

Aligning what the system should be

Workshops with executives across several seniority levels to map needs, pains, and optimal workflows. The core discovery: every level of the organization had a different mental model of what the CRM was for. Executives wanted a sales dashboard. Managers wanted team visibility. Frontline staff wanted a faster way to look up client data without switching tools.

User shadowing

Observing the actual workflow

Observed credit executives using the legacy systems in-branch. The key finding: a single client interaction required switching between 3+ tools, each with its own command syntax. The cognitive overhead wasn't just inefficiency — it was a source of errors that had compliance implications.

Empathy mapping & JTBD

What each role actually needed

Mapped the primary JTBD for each user type. The one that shaped the architecture most: "Quickly access full client history to identify the right cross-sell." The legacy system made this a 4-step process across 3 applications. The design goal was to make it a single view.

The international team dimension

The UI team was multinational — including Alicia Milessi, a UI designer who joined from Poland. Working across design perspectives from different markets sharpened the need for a clear shared vocabulary: not just design tokens, but alignment on what "ease of use" actually meant for a Mexican bank employee with a full client queue and a compliance obligation. The common language was the research output — the empathy maps and JTBD frameworks became the reference point that kept everyone aligned across cultural and disciplinary differences.

03 · Design

Three sprints. One system. Built with the people who had to use it.

[My call] The design process was structured as three Agile sprints, each with a specific scope and a defined stakeholder checkpoint before advancing. This wasn't aesthetic preference — it was a mechanism to ensure compliance and operational requirements were embedded at each stage, not retrofitted at the end.

Sprint 1

Map and co-design structure

Mapped existing workflows and co-designed the new CRM information architecture with stakeholders from all levels. No wireframes yet — just flow diagrams and IA validation with real users. The output: agreement on what modules existed, what they were called, and who could see what.

Sprint 2

Prototype and test core modules

Prototyped the client dashboard and credit simulator — the two most-used and most-complained-about modules — and tested them with credit executives. Findings fed directly back into the design: the initial credit simulator layout assumed users would navigate linearly; observation showed they jumped between sections constantly.

Sprint 3

Refine for accessibility and task efficiency

Refined the full interface based on Sprint 2 feedback, with specific focus on accessibility (CNBV-adjacent requirements) and task efficiency — measured as time-on-task for the 5 most common credit executive workflows. Command-based inputs were replaced with visual dropdowns and searchable histories throughout.

Key design principles applied

Three principles governed every design decision and served as the filter for any proposed addition:

Consistency: All modules unified under a single design system — shared navigation, shared typography, shared interaction patterns. A user who learned the client dashboard could transfer that knowledge directly to the credit simulator without relearning.

Efficiency: Command-based inputs (the legacy interface required memorizing codes) replaced with visual workflows: dropdowns, searchable client histories, contextual recommendations. [My call] This wasn't just a usability improvement — it was a compliance improvement. Fewer memorized codes meant fewer input errors in client records.

Cross-sell prompts: Contextual product recommendations integrated into the client view based on client behavior and portfolio position. The recommendation appeared at the moment of highest intent — when the executive was already looking at the client's full profile.

Regional director view

The CRM served multiple role levels with very different information needs. A credit executive needed granular client data. A regional director needed zone-level visibility — which branches were performing, which needed attention, without the operational noise of individual client records. [My call] The information architecture for the regional view was designed around this distinction: territory performance at a glance, drill-down only when needed.

04 · Regulatory & compliance work

Designing for compliance wasn't a constraint. It was part of the brief.

Santander Mexico operates under CNBV supervision and Grupo Santander's global IT security policies. Every design decision involving client financial data had to be validated against Internal Audit requirements — not as a post-design review, but as an integrated part of the sprint process.

I redesigned the CRM workflow in close collaboration with the Compliance and Internal Audit teams, ensuring that all data capture, client history access, and cross-selling flows met CNBV's customer data handling requirements. The information architecture for sensitive client financial data applied data minimization principles explicitly: role-appropriate information visibility, no cross-role data leakage, audit-trail integrity for every client record accessed.

Regulatory communication design

Beyond the interface itself, I developed tone-of-voice and communication guidelines for internal applications — ensuring that regulatory disclosures and compliance notices were clearly communicated to banking staff without creating operational friction. A compliance requirement that employees ignore because it's buried in dense legal language is worse than one stated simply and prominently.

05 · Results

The numbers.

+8% Cross-selling of credit products Contextual cross-sell prompts integrated into the client profile view drove an 8% increase in credit product sales — by surfacing the right recommendation at the moment of highest intent.
−30% New employee training time The intuitive interface eliminated the need to memorize legacy command codes. New executives reached operational competency 30% faster than with the previous system.
5+ Legacy tools replaced Credit simulators, transaction histories, portfolio trackers, training modules, and product catalogs — all unified into one consistent interface under a single design system.
100% Nationwide adoption Neo Jupiter was adopted across all Santander Mexico branches, replacing the legacy Jupiter system. The CRM continues in active use at the national level.
On getting stakeholder buy-in in a regulated environment

The most valuable research insight wasn't about the interface — it was about the decision-making process. In a regulated bank, design doesn't get approved by liking it. It gets approved by proving it doesn't introduce compliance risk. Running the stakeholder workshops early, involving Internal Audit in Sprint checkpoints, and framing every design decision in terms of its compliance implication shortened the approval cycle substantially. By the time Neo Jupiter reached final sign-off, every major stakeholder had already co-authored the system they were being asked to approve.
