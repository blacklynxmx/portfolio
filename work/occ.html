Case 01 / 06 · 2015 — 2021 · B2B SaaS · Consumer marketplace
OCC Mundial · part of SEEK Limited

Two audiences, one commerce engine.

Mexico's #1 job board had grown into a 20-year-old platform serving five million monthly users — and the same flow was trying to serve both the person looking for work and the business looking to hire. I split the research, rebuilt the product around a shopping cart, and set the groundwork for dynamic pricing users could never see.

Role Senior UX & Product Strategist
Duration 6 years · Full-time
Team 1 PO, 2 designers, 4 engineers, 2 analysts
Headline impact +34% revenue YoY, +15 NPS, 106% quota

01 · Before

The system we inherited.

OCC Mundial — officially Online Career Center — is Mexico's largest job board and part of SEEK Limited, the Australian group that also owns the leading boards in Brazil, Southeast Asia, Hong Kong, and elsewhere. By the time I joined in 2015 the business was profitable, the traffic was enormous — five million monthly users — and almost everything under the hood had been patched rather than rebuilt since around 2005.

The commerce engine was the clearest example. It treated candidates and recruiters as if they were symmetric. They aren't — and pretending they are is where twenty years of patching started to show.

Figure The job-ad admin as it stood when I arrived. Every recruiter — from a bakery owner hiring their first employee to an HR team running a 200-role pipeline — used this same screen.

Two audiences, one flow, and the logic didn't match either.

Candidates want to find a job fast, with as little friction as possible, and they want emotional reassurance that the effort is going somewhere. Recruiters want candidate quality, control over their spend, and tools that scale from one hire to a hundred. These two sets of jobs-to-be-done are adjacent — they live on the same marketplace — but they are not the same shape.

The existing commerce engine tried to flatten them. One product catalog. One pricing logic. One flow to create a job ad. Good enough when your recruiter base was homogeneous in 2005; painfully tight by 2015 when five different kinds of businesses were all trying to buy the same product for five different reasons.

The business reality

Revenue growth was slowing, but the diagnosis was murky. Was it pricing? Funnel leakage? Audience saturation? Wrong product mix? The leadership team had a hypothesis on each of these and not enough evidence to pick one. That's the moment research earns its keep — not to confirm a preferred answer, but to make the problem legible enough to decide.

The same screen was serving a bakery owner and an HR director running 200 hires. That's where 20 years of patching was catching up with us. — Diagnostic memo, early 2015

02 · Research

Two audiences, two tracks.

My first argument to leadership was structural. Most teams running research on a two-sided marketplace run one research practice covering both sides. That's where the mistakes accumulate — you end up with insights that generalize across audiences and product decisions that fit neither one cleanly.

[My call] I proposed splitting the research effort into two tracks that shared tooling but not conclusions. Track A would investigate candidates with consumer-research methods — JTBD, ethnography, emotional journey mapping. Track B would investigate recruiters with B2B segmentation methods — quantitative behavior analysis, stakeholder interviews, willingness-to-pay studies. Each track would own its own deliverables, and they would converge only at the product-decision layer.

Why I framed it this way

A two-sided marketplace has two kinds of truth. Candidate truth lives in emotion and behavior under pressure. Recruiter truth lives in operational constraints and unit economics. If you let one research program cover both, you'll find yourself reaching for whichever method is most comfortable — and you'll answer one side of the market with the wrong instruments. Keeping the tracks separate was the cheapest way to keep the methods honest.

Track A · Candidates (B2C)

What job seekers actually do under pressure.

Track A was built around one question: what does a job seeker do in the seven days between "I need to change something" and "I have a job offer"? Everything in the candidate-facing product — search, filters, alerts, the apply flow — should be tuned to that seven-day arc, not to the information architecture an engineering team finds tidy.

Method 01

JTBD interviews (n=10)

90-minute conversations with recently-hired or actively-searching job seekers across Mexico. Mapped the emotional arc from trigger event to accepted offer.

Method 02

Contextual inquiry

Shadowed participants during real search sessions. Measured what they did vs. what they said they did — the two rarely lined up.

Method 03

Survey (n=1,200)

Statistical validation of archetype frequencies, emotional states, and feature-importance rankings across the candidate base.

Method 04

Emotional journey map

Synthesized the arc into a single reference artifact — adopted as the team lens for all candidate-facing decisions.

Figure The ten participants from Track A interviews, synthesized onto a single reference. Each figure captures one real participant's trigger event, behavior pattern, and emotional state at the moment of search.

Three archetypes, one stress-test persona.

The ten participants clustered into three archetypes along two axes: clarity about what they wanted, and pressure to find it fast. Rather than design for a "primary persona" — a decision I've watched other teams regret — we used Fernanda, the inexperienced archetype, as the stress-test. If she could navigate a new feature, so could the other two. If she couldn't, we had work to do.

Track B · Recruiters (B2B)

Five recruiter types buying the same product for five different reasons.

[My call] Track B started from the other end. Before interviewing anyone, I pulled three years of transactional data and ran segmentation on behavior — not on stated industry. The question wasn't "what kind of company are you" but "how do you actually buy." Those two questions return different answers, and the second one is what the product has to solve.

Five segments emerged. Each had a distinct purchase cadence, price sensitivity, and support need. A single pricing model couldn't serve all five without over-serving some and under-serving the rest — which is what the legacy product was doing.

Micro businesses
1–10 employees. Hire 1–3 times per year. Self-service only — will not talk to sales. Need the cheapest ad that works. Primary failure mode: abandoning checkout at price reveal.
SME
11–250 employees. One or two HR people wearing six hats. Open to sales contact but time-poor. Primary need: a product bundle that feels decided-for-them. Willing to pay for the decision.
Corporate
250+ employees. Dedicated HR team, procurement process, requisition workflows. Primary need: volume, reporting, invoicing that matches their PO system. Sales-assisted purchase is the norm.
HR firms
Recruiting agencies reselling job ads and candidate access to their own clients. Intermediate actors. Primary need: white-label-ish transparency, volume pricing, fast account reload.
Massive recruitment
Retail, BPOs, delivery platforms. Hire hundreds per month. Primary need: throughput. Price per hire matters more than brand perception. Built-for-purpose pricing and admin tooling.
What the segmentation changed

Once the five segments were articulated with behavior data behind them, the product roadmap rewrote itself. Features that had been on the "nice to have" backlog turned out to be hard requirements for one segment (e.g., requisition-style PO workflows for Corporate). Features that had been planned as defaults turned out to be over-engineered for Micro. The segmentation wasn't a slide. It was the filter.

03 · Roadmap

CAJA — Create A Job Ad, as a shopping cart.

With two tracks of research converging, the product question became: what single flow lets a one-person bakery and a 200-hire HR team both buy what they need without feeling like the tool was designed for the other one? The answer landed in the most boring place possible — a shopping cart.

CAJA — Create A Job Ad — was the internal name for the new commerce flow. The metaphor was deliberately the one everybody already knows: add things to a cart, adjust quantities, apply any codes, check out. No configurator, no bundled plan, no sales hand-off required. Every recruiter segment, from Micro to Massive, buys the same way — with a different basket.

Why a cart, not a configurator

A configurator forces you to pre-declare your scenario: "I am X kind of business with Y need." That's exactly the kind of question no buyer wants to answer to a website. A cart lets you add and remove until the total feels right, which is how humans actually decide. It's also kinder to engineering: a cart is a small, well-understood state machine; a configurator branches combinatorially. The cart won on both ergonomics and build cost.

Figure The four-phase roadmap presented to leadership. Each phase was scoped so that the phase below it delivered standalone value — a discipline that saved us twice when budget cycles tightened mid-FY20.

Phase 01

Phase 02

Evolution — Flex Pricing research.

Once CAJA was live, the natural next question arrived from SEEK: could we introduce dynamic pricing — the kind that had worked elsewhere in the portfolio — without breaking user trust in a market where "variable pricing" carries a different cultural weight than in Australia or Southeast Asia?

[My call] The hard constraint

The instruction from leadership was explicit: "Users must never understand they are being priced dynamically. Every price must feel standard to the user." That single sentence shaped the entire research methodology — I couldn't ask direct questions about price fairness, couldn't A/B test pricing visibly, couldn't even use the word "dynamic" in a survey. The methodology had to work inside that constraint without breaking it.

Phase 01 · Quantitative

Historical data analysis

Three years of transaction data run through elasticity modeling by segment, by month, by product tier. Identified three distinct elasticity bands and the macro variables that shifted each one (hiring seasonality, end-of-quarter rushes, specific industry events).

Phase 02 · Qualitative

Constrained-language validation

Survey and interview protocols designed with rigid linguistic rules: never mention "price," "dynamic," "variable," or "discount." Only ask about perceived fairness of the experience, comfort with product tiers, and whether anything felt "off." The signal we wanted lived in the absence of negative reaction.

Phase 03 · Operational

[Team] POC testing with guardrails

Quiet A/B test on a small cohort. Primary metric: conversion lift. Guardrail metrics: complaint rate, churn, brand sentiment pulse. No breakout in any guardrail — the test could continue. The output was a methodology ready for portfolio-wide rollout under the same constraint.

Why this matters for senior research work

Most research protocols assume you can ask the user what they think. Commercially sensitive research often can't — either because the question biases the answer, or because asking the question creates a problem you didn't have. The Flex Pricing study was the piece of work I'm most likely to reach for when someone asks what senior research looks like in a commercial context: finding a way to get a defensible answer without letting the investigation become the story.

Beyond the core delivery

06 · Other proposals developed.

These are directions I proposed individually — research-backed, documented, and presented to leadership — that extended the CAJA delivery into adjacent product territory. None shipped as production features during my tenure; all three are included here as individual authorship. Screens for the recruiter backend are in design and will be published shortly.

[My call] Proposal A

Recruiter backend redesign. A proposal for a new recruiter workspace — job-ad builder, candidate inbox, real-time applicant tracking, and a performance dashboard — extending CAJA into a full self-serve admin experience. The research basis was the gap Track B surfaced: power recruiters were managing 40+ active ads through a UI designed for posting one at a time. I mapped the full task flow, defined the IA, and handed the concept to product leadership with a prioritised feature list and business case. Screens in design phase — publishing soon.

[My call] Proposal B

Self-serve recruiter analytics. A proposed analytics module letting recruiters see their own ad performance against segment benchmarks — without sales involvement. The insight that justified it: Track B segmentation showed that Micro and SME-segment recruiters never asked for performance reports because they didn't know reports were available, not because they didn't want them. Surfacing the data in-product, contextually, was a zero-cost retention play. I designed the data model, the disclosure logic, and the UI concept.

[My call] Proposal C

Dynamic pricing UI. The visible output of the Flex Pricing research from section 03. The constraint I worked within: users must never perceive they are being priced dynamically — surfacing the mechanism would destroy trust instantly. My proposal reframed contextual pricing as "recommended package for your segment" — the right price, presented as a recommendation, not a variable. I owned the UX rationale, the copy framing, and the interaction model.
