Case 06 / 07 · 2021 — 2025 · ResearchOps · CX Governance · Design System

You can't scale research by doing more research. You scale it by teaching others to do it.

The Compartamos loan origination case — how an 8-day process became 2 hours — is documented separately. This is the other half of that engagement: how the organization went from one UX researcher doing everything, to a distributed research practice where PMs, designers, and field coordinators could run validated studies independently. 300% capacity gain. Zero additional headcount in the research function.

01 · The problem

One researcher. Every product decision. No infrastructure to support either.

When I joined Compartamos, there was no research practice — there was a researcher. Me. My manager had hired me knowing that the scale of the digital transformation required more than one person could deliver alone, but the resourcing hadn't caught up. The immediate pressure was the loan origination digitization (detailed in the companion case). But behind that pressure was a structural problem: how do you make research systematic in an organization that has never had it?

The answer wasn't to hire a research team — the org wasn't structured for that, and the timeline for the fiscal year commitments didn't allow it. The answer was to design the research infrastructure so that others could contribute to it without becoming researchers themselves.

The constraint that shaped the solution

Every study, every VoC touchpoint, every data collection method had to pass through compliance review. Data minimization, informed consent, DPO sign-off — these weren't optional in a CNBV-supervised microfinance bank. The research infrastructure I built wasn't just a toolkit. It was a compliance-approved research toolkit, which meant every template, every protocol, every storage mechanism had been reviewed and cleared before a single PM ran their first test.

02 · Research toolkit

The first toolkit the organization ever had.

[My call] The toolkit wasn't a document. It was a system: a repository of pre-approved study templates, a panel management protocol, a synthesis framework, and a set of decision trees that helped a PM answer "which method do I use for this question?" without needing to consult a researcher first.

Study templates

Pre-approved, ready to run

Unmoderated usability test scripts for Maze and Useberry, pre-cleared by compliance. Moderated interview guides for 5 recurring research questions (onboarding, payment flow, error recovery, feature discovery, field agent workflow). Each template included consent language approved by the DPO — so a PM could run a study without triggering a new compliance review cycle.

Method selection

Right tool for each question

A decision framework — not a flowchart, an actual decision document — that mapped research questions to methods. "Does this prototype work as intended?" → unmoderated usability test. "Why are field agents abandoning this step?" → contextual inquiry. "Which of these two designs performs better?" → A/B test with minimum sample size calculator included. The framework reduced the most common failure mode: using a survey when you needed an interview.

Training program

Non-researchers running real studies

Trained Product Managers and Designers to run unmoderated usability tests independently in Maze/Useberry. The training wasn't a workshop — it was a supervised practice: they ran their first study with me reviewing the protocol, their second with me reviewing only the synthesis, their third independently with a quality check on the report. Three sessions to operational independence.

The 300% figure

At the start: 1 researcher (me) running all studies. End state: 1 researcher running strategic and high-complexity studies, plus 12 PMs and designers running independent unmoderated studies across 4 product squads. The number of research cycles per quarter went from roughly 4 to 16. The research function didn't grow — the research capacity did.

03 · Voice of Customer program

NPS is a number. VoC is a system that makes the number useful.

[My call] The VoC program integrated three measurement layers — NPS, CSAT, and CES — across the mobile app, the Contact Center, and the field agent channel. The key design decision was not what to measure, but when and how to connect the measurements to the product roadmap.

Most VoC programs generate reports. This one generated roadmap inputs. Every measurement cycle produced a ranked list of friction points with supporting qualitative evidence. Every quarter, that list went directly to the Engineering and Business Directors in a structured share-out that translated user friction into backlog items — not as recommendations, but as prioritized decisions with evidence attached.

The compliance dimension of VoC

Every data collection touchpoint in the VoC program — survey triggers, response storage, data retention periods, anonymization protocols — was designed in collaboration with the DPO team and approved before deployment. In a CNBV-regulated environment, collecting customer satisfaction data without proper consent architecture isn't just a privacy issue — it's a regulatory one. The program's data architecture was reviewed and approved before the first survey went live.

The regional satisfaction dashboards from this program are documented in the companion case study — showing the Toluca vs. Jalisco split that identified the field-to-Contact-Center handoff as the persistent weak point in the digital origination flow.

04 · CX governance

15,000 people delivering the experience. One framework making it consistent.

Compartamos's customer experience is omnichannel in the most literal sense: a customer might start a credit application with a field promotora, call the Contact Center to resolve a documentation question, and complete the process through the mobile app. Three completely different channels, different personnel, different systems — and until the CX governance framework existed, no shared standard for what that experience should feel like.

[My call] The CX governance framework defined four things: the interaction principles that applied across all channels, the escalation paths when a channel couldn't resolve a customer need, the measurement cadence for each channel's NPS/CSAT, and the language standards — including regulatory disclosure language — that ensured compliance requirements were met consistently regardless of which channel the customer was using.

Mobile app

Self-service with compliance guardrails

The app channel handled new credit origination, payment tracking, and account management. The governance framework defined which decisions the app could resolve autonomously (payment confirmation, document status) and which required escalation to field or Contact Center (disputed charges, risk exceptions). Every autonomous decision point had a compliance review.

Contact Center

Human resolution with structured protocols

The CC channel handled exceptions, complaints, and complex cases. The framework defined the call flow structure, the data lookup sequence, the duplicate record resolution protocol (documented in the companion case), and the tipification taxonomy — ensuring that every call outcome was recorded in a way that fed back into the VoC program and the field agent's CRM view.

Field agents

15,000+ promotoras and asesores

The field channel was the most complex to govern — not because of the technology, but because of the human variability. 15,000+ promotoras and asesores across urban and rural Mexico, operating in low-connectivity environments, with varying levels of digital fluency. The framework for this channel focused on decision authority (what can a field agent resolve vs. what requires back-office approval) and on the data capture standards that fed the digital origination flow.

05 · Design system

The DLS wasn't a deliverable. It was the thing that made all the other deliverables possible.

The design system for the Compartamos mobile app and Contact Center tool was built in parallel with the product — not as a separate workstream, but as the infrastructure that made consistent delivery possible across multiple squads working simultaneously on different product lines.

The foundation: a component library in Figma covering the mobile app (iOS and Android) and the web-based Contact Center tool, with documented usage rules, state variations, and accessibility annotations. The system was the shared reference that allowed the UI team — which included external contractors joining mid-project — to produce consistent interfaces without requiring a design review on every screen.

RBAC as a design system problem

[My call] One of the less visible but more complex DLS decisions was how to handle role-based access control in the component library. The same component — say, a client profile card — displayed different information and offered different actions depending on whether the viewer was a promotora, a Subgerente, or a Gerente Regional. Rather than designing three separate components, the DLS defined a single component with permission-aware states. Engineering implemented the state selection logic; the design system defined what each state looked like and what it could show.
