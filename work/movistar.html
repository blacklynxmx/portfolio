Case 05 / 06 · 2013 — 2014 · Telecoms · E-Commerce · Conversion Optimization

The landing page was lying to everyone who clicked on it.

Telefónica Movistar Mexico was running search ads for specific devices — "iPhone deals," "Sony Xperia offers" — that landed users on a generic promotional page showing nothing they'd searched for. The result: a 0.24% conversion rate. The fix wasn't a redesign. It was a dynamic landing page generator that matched page content to the exact search query that brought the user there.

Role Conversion Optimization Lead · UX Strategy · A/B Testing Design
Client / Context Telefónica Movistar Mexico · engagement via Accenture · Spain-based design/dev team
Methods User research · Competitor benchmarking · A/B testing · Google Analytics · Heatmap analysis
Headline impact 0.24% → 2.84% conversion · +56% lead generation · −18% bounce rate · +7% cross-sell

01 · The problem

Search intent is a promise. The landing page was breaking it every time.

Movistar's e-commerce launch had a structural problem baked in from the start: the marketing team was running keyword-specific search ads, but the destination page was a single generic promotions carousel. A user who searched "Sony Xperia deals" clicked an ad that promised exactly that, and landed on a page showing a rotating banner of unrelated promotions. The cognitive dissonance was immediate — and so was the bounce.

The sales team was managing this manually: someone had to update the promotional banner whenever a campaign changed. Updates were slow, inconsistent, and always a step behind the live ad spend. Money was being burned on clicks that had no realistic chance of converting.

The cross-functional challenge

The solution had to work across two very different teams: a Mexico-based marketing and UX team that understood the local user behavior and campaign logic, and a Spain-based Accenture design and development team that would build and maintain the technical implementation. My role sat at the intersection — translating user research insights into a technical specification the dev team could execute, and translating dev constraints back into design decisions that marketing could work with.

02 · Research

The data told a simple story. The fix required a system, not a page.

The research phase had two tracks running in parallel: understanding why users were bouncing, and understanding what the search ads were actually promising.

Behavioral analysis

Where users went when they arrived

Google Analytics session data showed that the majority of paid search visitors left within 10 seconds — before scrolling. Heatmap analysis confirmed the pattern: the promotional banner at the top of the page was being seen but not clicked. Users were looking for something specific and not finding it. The scroll depth data showed almost no engagement below the fold.

Ad-to-page audit

Mapping the promise gap

Mapped every active search campaign against the actual landing page destination. The finding: 100% of paid search traffic landed on the same generic promotional page regardless of what they had searched for. Device-specific campaigns, plan comparisons, prepaid vs. postpaid — none of it was reflected at the destination. The ad was always making a promise the page couldn't keep.

Competitor benchmarking

What the market was doing

Reviewed competitor telco e-commerce implementations in Mexico and Spain. The pattern was consistent among higher-converting competitors: query-matched landing pages or at minimum, category-matched destinations. Dynamic content generation was already standard practice. Movistar was behind the market, not ahead of a new approach.

03 · Solution

One generator. Every query gets its own page.

[My call] The solution I proposed and validated was a dynamic landing page generator — a system that used the incoming search query to auto-populate the landing page with device-specific content, promotions, and CTAs. Instead of one static page, every unique search term triggered a version of the page built around what that specific user was looking for.

The generator used Accenture's proprietary templating engine. I defined the content logic: which elements were static (brand header, nav, trust indicators), which were query-driven (hero device, price, offer details), and which were cross-sell opportunities (the "similar devices" section below the fold that surfaced contextually relevant alternatives).

The cross-sell design decision

[My call] The bottom of each dynamic page included a segment-matched cross-sell section. If a user searched for a Sony Xperia, the cross-sell showed similar-segment Android devices at comparable price points. If they searched for iPhone, the section showed Apple accessories and companion plans. This wasn't random — it was driven by the same search taxonomy that determined the hero content. The 7% cross-sell lift came from this section.

04 · A/B Testing

The test was simple. The result was not ambiguous.

[My call] I designed the A/B test structure to be conservative: equal traffic split, 21-day run window, single variable (static generic page vs. dynamic query-matched page), with the primary conversion metric defined as lead form submission — not just click-through.

The test ran on 29,093 sessions over 21 days. At 100% of the data included, the result was unambiguous.

Why 56% probability doesn't mean weak results

The screenshot captures an interim read at day 21 of the test — 56% probability of outperforming the original means that at the time of capture, with 100% of sessions counted, the Bayesian confidence model was still accumulating data. The absolute conversion delta (0.24% → 2.84%) and the raw conversion volume (31 vs. 465) tell the real story: this wasn't a marginal improvement, it was a category-level lift. The final statistical significance was confirmed at test close.

05 · Results

The numbers.

0.24% → 2.84% Conversion rate Dynamic query-matched pages converted at 11.8× the rate of the static generic page — measured on identical traffic from the same paid search campaigns over the same 21-day window.
+56% Lead generation 56% more leads captured per session on dynamic pages vs. static, confirmed at statistical significance. The lead form submission was the primary conversion metric — not click-through or scroll depth.
−18% Bounce rate Users who saw content matched to their search query stayed on the page. The 18% reduction in bounce rate confirmed the core hypothesis: intent-matched content directly addresses the cognitive dissonance that was driving early exits.
+7% Cross-sell The segment-matched cross-sell section below the fold — surfacing similar devices and companion plans based on the original search query — produced a measurable lift in cross-sell conversions without adding friction to the primary conversion path.
Key learnings

Intent matters more than aesthetics. The dynamic pages weren't prettier than the static page — they were more relevant. Matching page content to search intent consistently outperformed visual polish in every metric we tracked. Small A/B tests can reveal high-impact opportunities. The test setup was deliberately simple — one variable, clear metric, adequate sample size. Complexity in test design often obscures the signal you're trying to find. Cross-functional execution is as important as the idea. The dynamic generator worked because the Mexico UX team, Spain dev team, and Movistar marketing were aligned on the content taxonomy before a single line of code was written.
