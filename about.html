How I got here.

I started out as a graphic designer who got curious about why buttons fail. That curiosity became a career. Early on, at Mexico's federal housing fund, I learned that you can't design a public-financial product by taste alone — you have to know the law that governs it, the director who signs off, and the citizen who'll actually try to use it.

From there I moved through advertising agencies working on enterprise telecom and banking (Movistar via Accenture, Santander via Leo Burnett), then spent six years at Mexico's leading job board rebuilding its commerce engine, then four years leading UX research for the largest microfinance bank in Latin America. Today I run product and research at an AI-powered BI company serving regulated enterprise clients.

The common thread: systems that are old, audiences that don't agree with each other, and stakeholders who need the compliance case as much as the design case.

What I actually do.

On any given week my work splits roughly into three things.

Research that's defensible. Moderated and unmoderated usability, in-depth interviews, ethnography, JTBD, segmentation, surveys, card sorting. Maze and Useberry for scale. Dovetail for synthesis. The goal isn't "we did research." The goal is that when Risk or Legal asks why a flow looks the way it does, I can point at the evidence and it survives the meeting.

Strategy that's executable. Service blueprints, customer journey maps, roadmapping, prioritization (RICE / MoSCoW), unit economics when relevant. I'm most useful when the strategy survives contact with engineering — which means I need to understand what's expensive to build, not just what's valuable to ship.

Alignment that sticks. Stakeholder workshops, cross-functional war rooms, research share-outs with directors. In regulated industries, good research dies in the last meeting if the person reading it doesn't see their own problem reflected. So I learn to speak Risk, Legal, Ops, Sales — well enough, at least, to not lose the argument.

How I think about the work.

Three things I've come to believe, hard.

One.

Segmentation is a research tool, not a marketing artifact. If your user segmentation isn't changing how the product gets built, it's decoration. The OCC recruiter segmentation I'll walk you through on the case study page wasn't for a sales deck — it was to decide which features had to exist at all.

Two.

In regulated industries, the compliance case and the usability case are the same case. Every time I've seen a product fail compliance review late, it was because someone tried to argue them separately. The digital KYC flow at Compartamos shipped on time because Risk, Legal, and UX sat in the same room from week one, not because we optimized the funnel.

Three.

The best research output is the one a PM can read on Tuesday and act on by Friday. Long reports are ego. Decision-ready artifacts — a one-pager, an annotated flow, a decision memo — are the product. I've built more research practices by removing ceremony than by adding it.

Beyond the spreadsheet.

I read a lot — fiction mostly. Coetzee, McCarthy, Auster, Baricco. I run D&D campaigns (3.5 ruleset, stubbornly) for a table that's been together for years. I draw, with academic realism as the base. Music tends toward rock and electronica. I'm working on a fantasy novel in the long evenings.

This stuff isn't separate from the work. Running a D&D campaign is, among other things, long-form service design with improvising players. A well-constructed novel is a case study in information architecture — the order information gets revealed matters. Drawing teaches you that detail is subordinate to structure. I didn't plan the overlap. It just kept showing up.
